Research Report · Recommended Design v2 · July 2026

PUBLIC INPUT

THE PUBLIC · open-source coalition VS THE LOBBYIST · fictional archetype of the closed-model lobby

A vertical rhythm-fighter where policy claims are boss attacks and on-beat taps are the public's rebuttals. This report answers three questions: what is the best possible gameplay, what are the strongest real claims and rebuttals, and how the two fit together — every line sourced, every mechanic argued from the rhythm-game canon.

The Verdict

  • 4 lanes, 4 verbs. Cut double-tap and swipe (the two highest-failure touch gestures). Keep tap, hold+release, chord, avoid-fake.
  • Loosen timing, skew it late. PERFECT −20/+25ms, GOOD −35/+55ms. The v1 ±35ms window was tighter than Friday Night Funkin'.
  • 13 levels, not 17. Merge the three brand-compute levels and two regulation levels. Every surviving battle is verbatim-sourced.
  • The miss-buff loop is genre-correct. Punch-Out tells + Hellsinger-style punishment validate the pack's core satire engine. Keep it.
  • The archetype is the right call. Fictional composite = stronger legal footing, wider source pool, and the final-boss joke still lands — with receipts.
  • Ship web + Android first. Political satire has an Apple problem (documented below). The genre's real home is the browser.
01 / GAMEPLAY RESEARCH

The best version of the loop

The canon has already solved every piece of this game. Parappa proves the call should be the input pattern — listening and reading the chart are the same act. Rhythm Heaven proves windows should skew late (human motor lag trends late) and that difficulty should come from escalating conditions, never new verbs. Punch-Out!! proves a boss feels like a fighter when it telegraphs — including fake tells that punish mashing. Metal: Hellsinger proves misses should audibly degrade the music. And Ace Attorney proves arguments become gameplay when claims decompose into atomic, pokeable statements answered by specific verbs.

Four verbs, four rhetorical moves

Tap
Interrupt

The universal verb. A jab that lands as a one-line rebuttal. Zero registration risk.

Hold + Release
Press the point

Ace Attorney's "Press" as touch input: sustained pressure, released exactly on beat for a heavy backlash hit.

2-Lane Chord
Dogpile

Coordinated public action — two lanes at once. Reserved for climax beats only, never filler.

Avoid Fakes
Don't fall for it

Red panic notes. Hitting one helps The Lobbyist. This is the Punch-Out fake tell — the mechanic that makes the boss argue back.

CUT — Double-tap: OS double-tap ambiguity + redundant with two charted taps. CUT — Swipe-down: highest-frustration gesture class on touch, and semantically vague as a rhetoric verb. If a fifth verb is ever needed, prototype on real devices first.

Juice checklist — what makes a rebuttal land

  • Hitstop: 2–4 frames on PERFECT, 6–8 on chord climaxes
  • Shake = reward: screen shake on hits only, never on misses
  • Sound confirms the verb: unique one-shot SFX per gesture (Rhythm Heaven's core law)
  • The buff looks earned: boss recoil on PERFECT, smirk + shield-up on MISS
  • Two-stage tells 1–1.5 beats before every fake note (Punch-Out model)
  • Combo decays, never zeroes, on GOOD — near-misses aren't failures
  • Losing is audible: music thins while a Lobbyist buff is active (Hellsinger model)
  • Verdict beat: every level closes with a crowd roar + gavel, not just a score screen
02 / SATIRE & DISTRIBUTION RESEARCH

Punching up, shipping smart

Two decades of protest games give a clear pattern: slapstick + policy focus + speed to the news cycle wins; realistic violence and personal cruelty lose. And there is a documented Apple problem. The genre's real home is the browser — which is also where this game's one-thumb, 45-second-level design already lives.

2008
Sock and Awe

Throw shoes at Bush — built in days after the Baghdad press conference. Tens of millions of throws; sold on eBay. Slapstick + news-cycle speed.

VIRAL WIN
2011
Phone Story

Molleindustria's supply-chain satire. Pulled from the App Store within hours under objectionable-content rules. Lived on via web/Android; the removal became the story.

PULLED → PRESS WIN
2013
Endgame: Syria

Newsgame about the Syrian conflict. Rejected by Apple three times for targeting a real government — the canonical Guideline 1.1 case study.

REJECTED ×3
2017
Corbyn Run

UK Labour-aligned endless runner. Policy-cartoon tone, party-adjacent distribution, real press pickup during the election cycle.

CAMPAIGN WIN
2014
Glorious Leader!

Kim Jong-un parody run-and-gun. Kickstarter collapsed after hacks/controversy; tone read as edgy-for-edgy rather than pointed.

BACKFIRED
2016–20
The Trump-game wave

Hundreds of punch/toss/whack apps. The slapstick ones were tolerated as a genre; the gory outliers drew takedowns and blowback. Volume proved demand; quality decided reception.

GENRE PROOF

Platform reality

Apple's Guideline 1.1 bars "defamatory, discriminatory, or mean-spirited content," with rejections historically aimed at apps targeting real individuals and governments (Endgame: Syria; Phone Story; Sweatshop HD). A narrow carve-out for "professional political satirists" has existed in guideline history — ambiguous by design. Google Play requires satire to be clearly recognizable as such but is materially more permissive. The Lobbyist archetype was chosen partly for this reason — a fictional composite of a documented policy posture, in the protected tradition of parody (Hustler v. Falwell) and transformative use, is a far stronger position than any named-likeness defense. Even so: web + itch.io + Android first, App Store as a later, lawyered stretch goal.

Do

  • Keep every hit an argument landing — comic bursts, stars, squash-and-stretch
  • Stay policy-focused: the target is a posture, not a person's body, family, or identity
  • Show receipts — the sourced codex is the moral license for the slapstick
  • Move fast when the policy news cycle spikes (Sock and Awe's whole lesson)
  • Let the boss be competent and confident — a worthy opponent flatters the player and sharpens the satire

Don't

  • No blood, gore, injury realism, or anything that reads as genuine harm
  • No fabricated quotes presented as real — labels (direct / near / paraphrase / event) ship in the UI
  • No punching down or sideways: no staff, family, junior employees, or private individuals
  • Don't re-anchor the archetype to one person in marketing copy — the composite is the point
  • Don't bet the launch on iOS approval
Precedent check: the AI-policy satire space has scattered itch.io experiments (p(Doom)1, You Are Generative AI, and similar), but nothing in this genre slot — a sourced, mobile-first satirical rhythm-fighter about AI policy is first-mover territory.
