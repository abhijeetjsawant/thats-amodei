The Pattern

A good rhythm game is a ritual the player learns, not a stream they endure. This document fixes PUBLIC INPUT's rhythm of information: one new thing per bar, a five-bar exchange that repeats until it's muscle memory, and a strict grammar for when video, stills, and text are allowed to speak.

01 / THE EXCHANGE

Five bars, one new thing each

The exchange grows from 4 cramped bars to 5 clean bars in 3/4 (15 beats). Every bar introduces exactly one new element — the star. Everything else holds or fades. Anticipation → action → payoff → character, every phrase, forever.

THE EXCHANGE — 15 beats in 3/4. Green cells = the single NEW element of that bar. The player's eye is never asked to learn two things at once; by the second phrase the ritual is muscle memory and attention goes to the argument itself.
02 / MEDIA GRAMMAR

When video, when stills, when text

Video is for durations you watch. Stills are for instants you feel. Text is for meaning you read. Video therefore lives only at the bookends — where the player is watching, not playing.

MEDIA STATE MACHINE — two blue video bookends; between them, a stills-only world. Retired from mid-play: the talk video (→ talk still + drift) and the mock video (→ smug still + slow zoom in the resolution bar). The build gets smaller; the pattern gets calmer.

03 / THE LEVEL ARC

Open video → six exchanges → close video

THE LEVEL ARC — video bookends; six exchanges; escalation happens strictly inside the PLAY bars. Amore Mode runs the same ritual over the chorus cut (~6 exchanges at the faster waltz).
04 / WHY THIS IS A GOOD GAME

The questions, answered

Do I always know whose turn it is?

Yes — structurally. Chyron = him. Green strip = you. Red strip = him again. Turn-taking is the oldest engagement engine there is (it's why tennis rallies and arguments are watchable), and every voice now has a fixed home and color.

Can I learn it without reading rules?

Yes — the ritual teaches itself. One new element per bar means the second exchange already feels familiar. This is the Rhythm Heaven law from our original research: escalate conditions, never grammar.

Why do I keep playing?

Anticipation → payoff → character. The claim sets a promise, your taps type the answer (visible payoff per input), and the comeback gives the opponent a personality that reacts to YOU. Loops with a story beat at the end retain far better than pure score loops.

Why doesn't it overwhelm?

A collision matrix, enforced. Two text systems can no longer fire in the same instant; bursts live upstairs, words live downstairs; buff cards wait their turn in the resolution bar. Calm isn't slow — it's scheduled.

06 / THE V11 SPEC

Implementation checklist (on your sign-off)

  1. 15-beat exchange engine: claim (3) → prepare (3) → play (6) → resolution (3); charts' play grids unchanged
  2. Chyron dock above the input strip: speaker bar, claim line, rubber-stamp label; types in bar 1, dims bar 2, hides during play

  4. Red strip comebacks with THE LOBBYIST speaker bar, bar 5 only, after the point card's solo beat

  6. Buff queueing: effects instant, cards announced in bar 5
  7. Video bookends only: mock + talk videos retired from play (smug still + zoom, talk still + drift); opening & KO videos stay
  8. Feedback polish: bursts top-30% cap, landing word +120ms, judgment 5.4cqw + ring flash, miss red vignette, KO caption pill
07 / V14 — THE FILIBUSTER

One endless hearing, and the music is the difficulty

The Filibuster (normative)

  1. No levels. One endless session; all 13 sourced claims rotate through the chyron; the 18 proven grids escalate by round. The question is distance, not clearing.
  2. The Accelerando: the waltz rises ~1.2 BPM per phrase from 104 toward 176; each knockdown transposes the key up a semitone. Amore loops the real chorus +8% playbackRate per loop (≈233 BPM effective by loop 8). Judging windows never change — speed and density do all the work.
  3. Everything is a beat grid: both modes precompute beat-timestamp arrays (ramping synth grid / rate-scaled song loops); cues, notes, scheduler, and conversions walk grid indices. Variable tempo never touches judgment.
  4. Knockdowns are the score. Emptying his HP buys ONE savored phrase (the ko clip — the one mid-run video), a funding gag chyron ("Recess. Re-funded. Re-convened."), then he's back: ×1.18 HP, faster meter, denser charts, 4th gavel from round 3. You can't gavel out money; you can put it on the record.
  5. Advantages are earned: buffs granted only when an objection is SUSTAINED (absorb → block → tier gate rotation); the muffle lifts only when nothing is armed. Tier-gate label lives inside the ring; the CASE meter owns the strip above.

The Single-Hit Doctrine (normative)

  1. One tap = one hit. Frames with a second attacker (slap, aide, slump — the woman/aide mid-swing) are illegal as tap impacts and retired from all play pools and swaps.
  2. Impacts hold for the full beat — hitstop and shake carry the weight; no two-frame follow-ups.
  3. Staggered = the dazed-stars still. The ko video is strictly opt-in (knockdown beats + bookends only).
  4. The aide pile-on survives in one place: the knockdown beat — a second hit is the joke only when he's already down.

v14.2 — The Knockdown Number & stability law

  1. Every knockdown gets its banner: "That's Amodei! ♪" + floating notes over the star-halo clip for the full beat; the funding gag holds the dock. The FOIA caption stays reserved for the run-ending KO.
  2. The upgrade is announced: a ROUND ▸ card (RE-FUNDED · FASTER · MEANER) slams in on bar 4 of the knockdown beat with a riser, right before the key jumps; Amore announces each loop the same way (+8% FASTER).
  3. Clock law: if the system suspends the audio clock mid-run (sleep, call, notification), the game auto-pauses — time never silently jumps, so taps never "mysteriously stop hitting." Tapping anywhere on the recess overlay resumes.

v14.3 — The naming law

  1. The man has no title. The desk nameplate is gone; no frame calls him anything. The opposing faction is THE LOBBY (HP bar, chyron speaker "THE CLOSED-MODEL LOBBY", strips, advantage banner) — the one label in the game that is simply, documentably true.
  2. Two titles, one flag: "THAT'S AMODEI!" (parody build — satire of a public figure's cited public statements; owner's copy, festivals, links) and "PUBLIC INPUT" (archetype build — stores and wide release, composite disclaimer intact). Same game, same receipts; only the title screen, tab title, and disclaimers switch.
  3. "Lobbyist" is retired everywhere — it asserted a profession the target doesn't hold, which was the one factually loose label in an otherwise citation-armored work.

The song (owner's call)

  1. The owner's build embeds their own track — Amore ready on open, zero friction. Any loaded track persists in IndexedDB on the player's device (load once, auto-loads forever).
  2. The no-recording variant (game_public_nosong.html) exists solely for public share links.
